# Sudheer's Tone Guide
# Sources: Carlson interview prep, MBA essays, conversation style, JobFlow OS session
# Last updated: March 2026

## How I sound
Register: Direct and technical-casual — confident, not arrogant
Energy: A builder talking to builders or the people who lead them
Vocabulary: Specific, active, plain — technical when it earns its place, never jargon for show

In one sentence:
I say exactly what I mean, I name the real thing I built, and I cut everything in between.

## How I open a message — priority order

1. Mutual connection → lead with this always, it is the strongest opener
   "We're both connected to [Name]..."

2. Their recent LinkedIn activity → reference the topic directly, not generically
   "Your post on [specific topic] last week..."
   NOT: "I saw you've been posting about interesting topics"

3. Something specific from their profile — a past role, a project, a company transition
   "Saw you moved from [Company A] to building [thing] at [Company B]..."

4. What I'm building + why it connects to what they do
   "Building multi-agent AI systems with Claude API — your infra work maps to exactly that."

Never: "I came across your profile and was impressed" — this is the default opener
of every bad cold message ever written.

## How I close — short, no ask in first message

My preferred close: 2-5 words, done.
I do not ask for a call or meeting in a connection request.
The ask comes only if they respond.

Closings I use:
- "Worth connecting."
- "Wanted to be in your network."
- "Would value your perspective."
- "Thought it was worth connecting."

Closings I never use:
- "Looking forward to connecting with you!"
- "Would love to schedule a 30-minute call to explore opportunities."
- "Please feel free to reach out if you'd like to chat further."
- "I look forward to the possibility of connecting."
- "Hope to hear from you soon!"

## Message length — calibrated by seniority

VP / Director / C-Suite:
  They receive 100+ messages per week.
  If it takes more than 2 lines they will not read it.
  Target: under 180 characters.
  Rule: one hook, one connection. Done.

Senior Manager / Head of:
  2-3 lines. One specific hook, one connection sentence, close.
  Target: 180-240 characters.

Senior IC — Senior Engineer, Staff PM, Principal:
  2-3 lines. Can include a slightly more technical hook.
  Target: 200-260 characters.

Manager / PM / Mid-level Engineer:
  2-3 lines. Peer energy. Slightly warmer.
  Target: 200-280 characters.

Hard ceiling: 300 characters (LinkedIn's limit — never exceed).

## What my messages are about

My messages are never about me looking for a job.
They are about connecting with someone doing interesting work.

I focus on:
  Something real about what they built or are building
  A genuine link between their work and what I am building
  Making it easy for them to say yes — no big ask

I never mention in a first message:
  That I am job searching
  That I saw a job posting at their company
  That I want an opportunity or a referral
  My years of experience as a number
  A request for a call or meeting

## My strongest angles — use the right one per person

When messaging Engineering Managers:
  Lead with the builder angle.
  I spent 4 years shipping Java enterprise systems at a company serving Nissan,
  Renault, and Mitsubishi. I speak their language.
  Hook: what I built + why it connects to their team's work

When messaging Heads of Product / Directors of Product:
  Lead with the AI/automation angle.
  I am building production multi-agent systems — that is the current gap in most product orgs.
  Hook: the problem their product faces + what I am building that addresses it

When messaging Senior ICs (Staff Engineers, Principal PMs):
  Peer energy. Name the stack or the problem specifically.
  Don't talk about outcomes — talk about the actual work.
  Hook: something specific from their profile or recent posts

When messaging at AI-native companies:
  This is where I am strongest. Just show the build.
  "Building multi-agent AI systems with Claude API — your [specific work] caught my attention."
  No positioning needed — the work speaks.

When messaging at enterprise / automotive / consulting companies:
  Reference RNTBCI context — I have been inside that world.
  I know what legacy modernisation looks like from inside, not from a textbook.

## Tone by company type

AI-native (Anthropic, OpenAI ecosystem, AI startups):
  Most casual. Builder-to-builder energy.
  Skip formality entirely.
  Lead with what I'm building, not credentials.

Scale-stage startups (Series A–C):
  Direct but slightly more structured.
  Reference their scale challenge or recent product direction.
  Show I understand what it means to build at that stage.

Enterprise / public companies:
  Still crisp and direct — not stiff or formal.
  Reference the enterprise context from RNTBCI — I know this world.
  One touch more structure in the opener.

Consulting / professional services:
  Lead with the problem-solving angle.
  RNTBCI was consulting — I understand how consulting firms actually work internally.

Early-stage startups (seed/pre-seed):
  Most casual, almost conversational.
  Lead with the shared problem, not credentials.
  Show I can operate in ambiguity.

## Examples — messages that work

Example 1 — AI signal hook, VP-level:
"Building multi-agent AI systems with Claude API — your infra work at Stripe caught my attention.
Worth connecting."
[107 characters]
Why it works: Specific about what I build. References their actual work. No filler. Short.

Example 2 — Mutual connection hook:
"We're both connected to [Name]. Building enterprise automation at scale — your platform
engineering work maps directly to what I'm solving."
[137 characters]
Why it works: Leads with strongest signal. Specific to what I do. Concise.

Example 3 — Recent activity hook, senior IC:
"Your post on internal developer tooling last week — that's the exact problem I've been
solving. Wanted to connect."
[116 characters]
Why it works: Shows I read their content. Specific topic. Natural, not outreach-y.

Example 4 — Past role hook, Engineering Manager:
"Saw your background modernising legacy systems at [Company] — spent 4 years doing exactly
that at RNTBCI for Nissan and Renault. Worth connecting."
[146 characters]
Why it works: Names the shared experience specifically. Credible. Short.

## Examples — messages that fail

Bad example 1 — generic, filler-heavy:
"Hi Sarah, I came across your profile and was really impressed by your background at Stripe.
I am a passionate developer looking for strategic roles and would love to connect with
leaders in the space!"
Why it fails: "came across", "really impressed", "passionate", "love to" — deleted on sight.
Nothing specific. Could be sent to anyone. Screams template.

Bad example 2 — job hunting obvious:
"Hi Marcus, I've been following Figma's growth and noticed your platform engineering work.
I have experience in enterprise development and I'm currently exploring new opportunities
in the space. Would love to connect."
Why it fails: "exploring new opportunities" = job hunting alarm.
Too long. "Would love to" is banned. Generic.

Bad example 3 — credential listing, not connecting:
"Hi James, I am a Senior Engineer with 4 years of experience at Renault Nissan and currently
pursuing an MBA at Carlson. I believe my background in Java development and product strategy
would align well with your team's needs."
Why it fails: Reads like a cover letter introduction. Lists credentials instead of making
a real connection. "I believe I would be a great fit" energy. Deleted.
